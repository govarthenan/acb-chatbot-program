ABSTRACT Embedding models are fundamental to modern Natural Language Processing (NLP),
transforming textual data into dense vector representations that capture semantic meaning and relationships.
This systematic survey focuses exclusively on the landscape of open-source embedding models, tracing
their evolution from early word embeddings (Word2Vec, GloVe) to advanced contextual and multimodal
architectures (BERT, Sentence-BERT, BGE, Nomic Embed). Moving beyond descriptive narratives, our
core contribution is a structured, comparative analysis of these models’ operational tradeoffs, including
model size, embedding dimensionality, inference latency, and memory consumption. This systematic review
spans the literature up to 2025 drawing upon 70 papers to provide a critical assessment of standardized
benchmarks, such as MTEB, and their limitations regarding bias and robustness. Finally, the paper concludes
by discussing the broad applicability of embedding models across diverse domains, including search engines,
recommendation systems, fraud detection, and healthcare, and offer recommendations for effective model
selection and utilization.

I. INTRODUCTION
Embedding models have become the cornerstone of modern
Natural Language Processing (NLP), serving as a funda-
mental layer for a wide array of downstream tasks [1].
These models function by transforming textual data, whether
individual words, short phrases, complete sentences, or entire
documents, into dense numerical vectors within a high-
dimensional space [2](see Figure 1).
The key principle behind this transformation is to capture
semantic meaning and the intricate relationships that exist
between different textual units [1]. By representing language
in this numerical format, embedding models enable machine
learning algorithms to efficiently process and understand the
nuances of human language [3].
The associate editor coordinating the review of this manuscript and
approving it for publication was Maria Chiara Caschera .
The significance of embedding models lies in their ability
to convert qualitative textual data into a quantitative form
that machines can readily interpret and manipulate [4]. This
numerical representation allows the application of mathe-
matical and statistical techniques to analyze and compare
textual information, which is crucial for tasks such as
semantic search, personalized recommendation, large-scale
information retrieval, text classification and clustering, and
question answering systems [5], [6]. The ability to represent
the meaning of text as numerical vectors has revolutionized
how machines interact with and understand human language.
The evolution of embedding models reflects a significant
paradigm shift in the field of NLP. Early approaches relied
on symbolic representations, treating words as discrete,
unrelated entities. The advent of word embeddings marked
a turning point, allowing semantic similarities to be captured
in a continuous vector space [1]. These embeddings enabled
algebraic operations on meaning, such as analogies and

As NLP tasks grew more complex, there was a need
to represent larger textual units. This gave rise to sen-
tence embeddings, where models like Sentence-BERT [10],
Universal Sentence Encoder [11], and MPNet [12], [13]
encapsulate the overall meaning of sentences or paragraphs.
More recently, multimodal embeddings have emerged, inte-

graphs) into unified representations. Open-source models
such as Nomic Embed Vision [14], [15] and OpenCLIP [16]

While these evolutionary milestones laid the groundwork
for today’s embedding landscape, the field is now under-
going a critical paradigm shift driven by efficiency and
accessibility. Previous surveys in the literature predominantly
focus on word embeddings or earlier, primitive forms of
representation learning, and often emphasize benchmark
performance in isolation. In contrast, this work provides
a systematic and forward-looking review of open-source
embedding models (< 2025). To the best of our knowledge,
this is the first comprehensive survey dedicated exclusively to
modern open-source embedding models. Beyond descriptive
performance reporting, we conduct a structured comparative
meta-analysis of critical operational trade-offs, including
model size, embedding dimensionality, memory footprint,
and inference latency. This approach provides practition-
ers with the crucial, resource-aware guidance needed for
effective deployment, positioning this manuscript as a
vital resource for both synthesizing recent developments
and shaping the future direction of open-source, efficient
embedding research.
A. DATA MODALITY
Embedding models today exhibit versatility across multiple
data modalities [17]. Text embeddings remain foundational,
spanning word-level [18], [19], [20], sentence-level [11],
[21], and document-level representations. Image embed-
dings, pioneered by models like CLIP and its open-source
counterpart OpenCLIP, leverage contrastive learning to align
images and text. In audio, models such as Wav2Vec 2.0
[22] and HuBERT [23] generate robust speech embeddings,
enabling tasks like classification, speaker identification, and

(GATs) [26], with applications in social networks, knowledge

B. EVALUATION AND BENCHMARKS
To evaluate and compare embedding models, the NLP
community relies on standardized benchmarks. Platforms
such as the Massive Text Embedding Benchmark (MTEB)
[30] provide a comprehensive suite of tasks, including
information retrieval, clustering, classification, and semantic
textual similarity, spanning multiple languages and domains.
Public leaderboards, notably those hosted by Hugging

embedding models across these tasks. It is important to note

model rankings and reported performance may evolve
significantly beyond this point in time. Despite their utility,

sensitivity to contextual variation remain central evaluation

trade-offs between open-source and proprietary embedding

C. SURVEY METHODOLOGY AND SCOPE
This manuscript is designed as a systematic, transparent, and
reproducible survey of open-source embedding models. The
scope is explicitly bounded to non-proprietary models and
publicly available scientific literature published or substan-
tially updated between 2013, marking the introduction of
modern vector embeddings with Word2Vec—and early 2025.
The survey traces the evolution from static word embeddings
to contextual, multimodal, and efficiency-oriented architec-
tures (e.g., BERT, SBERT, BGE, and Nomic).
Models included in this review must (i) be fully open-
source, with open model weights; (ii) demonstrate relevance
to general NLP or multimodal retrieval tasks; and (iii) report
empirical evaluations on widely adopted public benchmarks,
particularly MTEB or MMTEB, along with operational char-
acteristics such as model size, embedding dimensionality,
or inference latency. Proprietary or closed-source APIs are
excluded from in-depth technical analysis and are referenced
only for historical context or high-level comparison. Models
limited to highly specialized or narrow application domains
without broad research applicability are also excluded.
To ensure objectivity and reproducibility, a structured
systematic review workflow was followed. The guiding
research question is: How have open-source embedding
models evolved from static word vectors to contextual and
multimodal architectures, and what operational tradeoffs
govern their deployment? A comprehensive literature search
was conducted using targeted keywords, including ‘‘embed-
ding models’’, ‘‘open-source embedding models’’, ‘‘MTEB
benchmark evaluation’’, ‘‘contextual sentence embeddings’’,
‘‘word embeddings’’, and ‘‘multimodal retrieval-augmented
generation’’. Searches were performed across major aca-
demic and technical repositories, including but not limited
to google scholar, IEEE Xplore, arXiv, and Hugging Face,
yielding an initial set of 1,480 records. After deduplica-
tion (320 records removed) and title–abstract screening,
215 studies remained for full-text eligibility assessment.
A subsequent quality appraisal prioritized methodological
transparency, benchmark reproducibility, and clarity of
reported metrics, resulting in 110 eligible studies. From this
corpus, structured data were extracted to support comparative
analysis, including (i) operational tradeoffs such as parameter
count, embedding dimension, and inference latency; (ii)
architectural techniques, including subword modeling, self-
attention distillation, and Matryoshka learning; and (iii)
application contexts such as healthcare, fraud detection, and
retrieval-augmented generation. Following thematic consol-
idation and historical validation, the corpus was refined
to 70 core papers, which form the empirical foundation
of this survey. A comprehensive view can be seen in

D. CONTRIBUTIONS OF THIS PAPER
The primary contribution of this paper is to present
a forward-looking and comprehensive examination of
open-source embedding models, with emphasis on their per-
formance characteristics and practical applications. Moving
beyond a purely descriptive review of existing studies, this
work adopts a deployment-focused and analytically grounded
perspective on the open-source embedding landscape. Instead
of simply listing available models, benchmarks, and use
cases, we propose a structured framework that systematically
links model architecture, benchmark performance, and
real-world operational considerations. Specifically, we:
1) Trace the evolution of embedding models, from
early word embeddings (Word2Vec, GloVe, FastText)
to advanced contextual and multimodal approaches
(BERT, Sentence-BERT, MPNet, MiniLM, BGE, GTE,
and Nomic Embed).
2) Present a structured comparison of open-source embed-
ding models, highlighting their strengths, weaknesses,
and task suitability.
3) Review benchmarks such as MTEB and analyze their
role in measuring embedding effectiveness, alongside
limitations related to bias, dimensionality, and robust-
ness.
4) Establish a decision-oriented selection framework that
links model characteristics (e.g., contextual depth,
multilingual capability, long-context support, effi-
ciency mechanisms) to concrete application scenar-
ios, enabling informed and resource-conscious model
selection.
5) Explore practical applications across domains includ-
ing semantic search, recommender systems, fraud
detection, and healthcare.
6) Provide practical guidance for embedding model
selection and deployment, balancing performance,
interpretability, and resource constraints.

A. WORD EMEBBING MODELS
1) Word2Vec
Word2Vec [4], a pioneering model developed by Google,
learns word embeddings by analyzing the contexts in
which words appear. It employs two primary architec-

predicts a target word from its surrounding context, and
Skip-gram, which predicts the surrounding words given
a target word. Its ability to capture semantic relation-
ships by placing semantically similar words close together
in the embedding space made it a highly influential
model.

4) GLOBAL VECTORS FOR WORD REPRESENTATION -
GLOVE
Another well-established word embedding technique is
GloVe (Global Vectors for Word Representation), devel-
oped by researchers at Stanford University [19]. Unlike
Word2Vec’s local context focus, GloVe leverages the global
co-occurrence statistics of words within a corpus to learn
word embeddings. Its objective is to create a vector space
where the dot product between the vectors of two words is
related to the logarithm of their co-occurrence probability.
This has proven to be particularly effective in capturing
semantic relationships and performing well in tasks such
as word analogy [34]. GloVe constructs a word-word co-
occurrence matrix X, where Xij represents the number of
times word j appears in the context of word i. The model aims
to learn word vectors vi and vj such that their dot product
approximates the logarithm of the words’ co-occurrence
frequency.

B. TEXT EMBEDDING MODELS
1) FastText
FastText, created by Facebook, extends the Word2Vec
model by incorporating subword information [35]. Instead
of treating words as atomic units, it breaks them down
into character n-grams and learns embeddings for these
subword components. The embedding for a word is then
represented as the sum of the embeddings of its constituent
n-grams. This approach allows FastText to handle out-of-
vocabulary words and capture morphological information,
making it particularly advantageous for morphologically rich
languages [36].
Moving beyond word-level representations, transformer-
based models have revolutionized the field of embedding
models.
2) BIDIRECTIONAL ENCODER REPRESENTATIONS FROM
TRANSFORMERS
BERT, developed by Google, is a prime example [37].
It generates contextualized word embeddings by analyzing
the entire sequence of words in both left and right directions,
leveraging the encoder segment of the transformer architec-
ture and being pre-trained on large-scale text corpora using
masked language modeling and next sentence prediction
objectives [38]. This bi-directionality and the transformer’s
attention mechanism enable it to understand the context of
words in a sentence more effectively than previous models.
The core of the transformer is the scaled dot-product
attention, calculated as:

3) SENTENCE-BERT
While BERT excels at generating contextualized word
embeddings, SBERT is a framework specifically designed
to produce semantically meaningful embeddings for entire
sentences. SBERT builds upon pre-trained BERT networks
by adding a pooling layer to the output and then fine-tuning
the model on sentence pair tasks using Siamese or triplet
network structures [21]. This fine-tuning process optimizes
the model to produce sentence embeddings that can be
effectively compared using cosine similarity, making SBERT
highly suitable for tasks like semantic search and text
similarity.
4) MASKED AND PERMUTED PRE-TRAINING FOR
LANGUAGE UNDERSTANDING
MPNet, developed by Microsoft Research Asia, represents
a further advancement in pre-training techniques [12].
It combines the strengths of BERT’s masked language
modeling (MLM) and XLNet’s permuted language modeling
(PLM) while addressing their limitations [39]. In MLM,
a certain percentage (e.g., 15%) of the input words are
randomly masked, and the model’s task is to predict the
masked words based on the context. This forces the model
to understand the context in both directions (left and right),
leading to powerful contextualized representations. MPNet
leverages the dependency among predicted tokens through
permuted language modeling and takes auxiliary position
information as input to reduce the discrepancy between
pre-training and fine-tuning. This innovative approach allows
the model to achieve strong performance on a variety of
language understanding tasks.
5) MiniLM
For applications requiring efficient embedding generation
without sacrificing much in performance, MiniLM presents
a compelling solution [40]. Developed through a process
known as deep self-attention distillation, MiniLM is a
distilled version of large transformer-based models like
BERT [40], [41]. This technique trains a compact ‘‘student’’
model to replicate the self-attention behavior of the last
transformer layer of a larger ‘‘teacher’’ model. The result is a
model that is significantly smaller and faster, yet still retains
much of the performance of its teacher.
6) BAAI GENERAL EMBEDDING
Another powerful family of text embedding models is
BGE (BAAI General Embedding), developed by the Beijing
Academy of Artificial Intelligence [42]. They are known for
their versatility and strong performance across various NLP
tasks. BGE models—such as BGE-M3—support multiple
retrieval paradigms, including dense, multi-vector, and sparse
retrieval. They also offer impressive multilingual capabilities,
supporting over 100 languages, and are capable of handling
input sequences up to 8192 tokens, making them suitable for
both short and long-form text.
7) GENERAL TEXT EMBEDDING
Similarly, the GTE (General Text Embedding) models
from Alibaba Group stand out for their performance in
multilingual text embedding and retrieval [43]. Models
like gte-multilingual-base excel in multilingual retrieval
benchmarks, while others such as GTE-Qwen2-7B-instruct
and gte-large-en-v1.5 rank highly on the MTEB leaderboard.
The latter is specifically optimized for English tasks and can
process very long inputs, up to 8192 tokens.
8) NOMIC EMBED MODELS
Finally, Nomic Embed, developed by Nomic AI, offers
efficient and high-performing models for both text and
multimodal embeddings [44]. Nomic Embed Text achieves
strong results compared to proprietary models like OpenAI’s
ada-002 and text-embedding-3-small, while maintaining a

C. MULTIMODAL EMBEDDING MODELS
Multimodal embedding models represent a shift from
single-modality processing to a unified semantic space where
different data types, such as text, images, and audio, are
mapped to a shared coordinate system. This alignment allows
the model to ‘‘understand’’ that the text ‘‘a golden retriever
in the park’’ and an image of the same scene occupy the
same semantic neighborhood. The architectural foundation
of multimodal embeddings has evolved from special-
ized, rigid structures to flexible, transformer-based unified
encoders:
1) CONTRASTIVE DUAL-ENCODERS
This architecture uses two distinct backbones (e.g., a Vision
Transformer for images and a BERT-based model for text).
The models are trained using contrastive learning to pull
‘‘positive’’ pairs (matching image and text) together while
pushing ‘‘negative’’ pairs apart.
2) UNIFIED MULTIMODAL LLMs
A significant challenge in multimodal embeddings is han-
dling varying input lengths and resolutions. Nomic Embed
Vision [14] addresses this by providing high-performance,
open-source embeddings that support long-context text and
unified visual-textual search. By employing techniques like
Matryoshka Representation Learning (MRL), Nomic allows
for flexible embedding dimensions, enabling users to truncate
vectors for efficiency—a concept central to the ‘‘New
Frontiers’’ of retrieval performance.
3) E5 AND mmE5
The E5 (Emphasized Embedding) family introduced the
concept of utilizing weakly supervised data followed by
fine-tuning on high-quality synthetic data. The recent mul-
timodal E5 mmE5 [45] extends this to multimodality, using
a ‘‘deep thinking’’ pass of a multimodal LLM to gener-
ate synthetic alignment data. This architectural approach
treats the embedding task as a specialized distillation of
a much larger multimodal LLM, resulting in state-of-the-
art performance on the MMEB (Multimodal Embedding
Benchmark).
4) CLIP (CONTRASTIVE LANGUAGE-IMAGE PRE-TRAINING)
CLIP [46] revolutionized the field by demonstrating that sim-
ple contrastive learning on a massive scale (400 million pairs)
could create robust, zero-shot multimodal representations.
It effectively proved that a dual-encoder architecture—
consisting of a modified ResNet or ViT for vision and
a Transformer for text—could learn highly transferable
features without task-specific fine-tuning.
The progression from static, global embeddings
(Word2Vec, GloVe) to dynamic, contextual representa-
tions (BERT, SBERT, MPNet) and finally to specialized,
efficiency-focused models (MiniLM, BGE, GTE, Nomic
Embed) marks the key evolutionary steps in the field. This
diversity compels practitioners to carefully weigh model
performance against deployment constraints. As summarized
in Table 1, the decision necessitates a systematic analysis
of the inherent Efficiency Tradeoffs, where achieving peak
performance and long-context capabilities often correlates
with higher latency and memory demands, while lightweight
models like MiniLM and Nomic Embed offer optimized
alternatives for resource-constrained environments. For
example, the latency and memory descriptors in Table 1,
are relative estimates informed by common deployment
observations rather than a single unified protocol. In practice,
‘‘Low’’ corresponds to inference times on the order of
tens of milliseconds per embedding and memory footprints
typically below ∼1 GB (e.g., All-MiniLM-L6-v2 on CPU).
‘‘Moderate’’ indicates average latencies in the 100–300ms
range with peak memory often around ∼1–2GB , while
‘‘High’’ refers to larger models with hundreds of milliseconds
to over one second per query and memory demands exceeding
∼2–3GB on typical hardware. These bands reflect consistent
patterns seen across community benchmarks for open-source
embedding models.
III. TRAINING DATA AND DATASETS
A. COMMON CRAWL INITIATIVE
The Common Crawl initiative is a major open repository of
web crawl data that has supported research and development
across fields since its launch in 2008 [47]. It has archived
petabytes of information, including raw web pages, metadata,
and text extracts [48], offering a longitudinal view of the inter-
net’s evolving structure and content. Accessibility remains a
core principle, with datasets stored on Amazon Web Services’
Public Data Sets and available without requiring an AWS
account. As of 2025, the dataset includes documents in many
languages, though English dominates at about 60% [48],
[49]. It also includes millions of PDFs and diverse web
content, organized into three file types: WARC (full content),
WAT (metadata), and WET (extracted text) [47], allowing
flexible access based on research needs. Common Crawl
data has broad applications, such as training natural language
processing models, conducting computational linguistics
research, performing market trend analyses, and studying
cultural and political movements. Furthermore, the regular
releases of host- and domain-level graphs provide deeper
insights into the web’s interconnected structure.
Nevertheless, the dataset has limitations. The automated
crawl process tends to prioritize frequently linked domains,
leading to an over-representation of popular websites and
a lack of coverage for digitally marginalized communities.
Language distribution is heavily skewed toward English,
which may introduce biases into downstream tasks like
word embedding training. Additionally, Common Crawl

B. WIKIPEDIA AND PROJECT GUTENBER
Wikipedia, a widely popular online encyclopedia, has rapidly
grown since its inception to encompass an extraordinarily
large corpus with broad scope and constant updates. Available
under the GNU Free Documentation License [50], it offers a
vast amount of freely accessible text, making it a valuable
resource for training word embeddings. The English version
of Wikipedia alone contains millions of articles, and the
platform supports over 300 different languages, making it
ideal for the collection of sample texts for multilingual
corpora [51], [52]. The articles within Wikipedia generally
adhere to a consistent structure and are organized into
category hierarchies, which can aid in learning semantic
relationships within specific domains.
However, the collaborative editing nature of Wikipedia
also presents challenges. The accuracy of its content is
frequently questioned, and systemic bias means that quality
and coverage can be uneven [53]. Variations in English
dialects, for instance, can introduce differences in semantics,
diction, and spelling. The sheer volume of text, exceeding
a terabyte for the English version, necessitates efficient
processing techniques [54]. While it strives for a neutral
point of view (NPOV), political bias has been observed,
with sentiment analysis suggesting a tendency to associate
right-of-center public figures with more negative sentiment
compared to left-of-center figures. Bias can also manifest
in the portrayal of certain topics across different language
versions, such as the negative emphasis on transgender
individuals in Russian Wikipedia compared to English or
French versions [55]. Subjectivity and framing bias, where
an author’s perspective is presented as absolute truth, can
also be present. The lack of diversity within the Wikipedia
editor community, which is predominantly white and male,
is another factor that can contribute to biases in the
content [56], [57]. Furthermore, the corpus contains noise in
the form of contradictions, vandalism, and edits unrelated to
bias mitigation.
Project Gutenberg on the other hand, is a long-standing
volunteer effort focused on digitizing and archiving cultural
works, primarily consisting of copyright-free literary works
(books). Founded in 1971, it is the oldest digital library, with a
collection exceeding 70,000 items. While primarily featuring
literary fiction, it also includes non-fiction titles such as
biographies, histories, and reference works. The majority of
its releases are in the English language, but a significant
number of non-English works are also available. The standard
format for texts in Project Gutenberg is plain text, commonly

IV. NEW FRONTIER IN MULTIMODAL EMBEDDING:
EFFICIENCY AND UNIFICATION
The quest for computational efficiency in multimodal embed-
dings is no longer a secondary concern but a primary design
principle. Recent architectural innovations are fundamentally
changing how these representations are created and utilized.
A core strategy is dimensionality reduction [58], which
allows for the representation of high-dimensional data,
such as images where each pixel is a dimension, into a
lower-dimensional space. This process, by identifying com-
monalities and patterns, reduces the computational power and
time required for deep-learning models to learn and infer
from data.
A notable technique that embodies this principle is the
Matryoshka embedding [59], [60]. Models like Voyage-3-
large and Nomic Embed Vision utilize this approach, which
is designed to concentrate the most important semantic
information within the initial dimensions of the output vector.
This allows the vector to be truncated to a smaller size without
a significant loss of semantic information, thereby balancing
high performance with lower inference costs and faster search
times due to a reduced search space.
Another critical innovation addresses the data bottleneck.
Multimodal Large Language Models (LLMs) are now being
leveraged to generate high-quality synthetic datasets that
cover a wide range of tasks and modality combinations [61],
[62]. This addresses a significant historical challenge: the
limited availability of labeled multimodal data, which has
often hindered embedding model performance. The mmE5
model [45] is a prime example of this approach, achieving
state-of-the-art performance on the MMEB Benchmark by
training on a large-scale, multilingual dataset synthesized via
a deep thinking process within a single pass of a multimodal
LLM.
The evidence points to two distinct, yet complementary,
approaches to enhancing multimodal embedding efficiency.
The first, exemplified by mmE5, focuses on improving the
quality of the training data to build a more robust and
accurate traditional embedding model. This is a classical
machine learning strategy where a superior input leads to
a superior output. The second, which is more radical, re-
architects the retrieval process itself to bypass the inherent
scalability limitations of traditional embedding-based search.
This intellectual divergence suggests that the future of
multimodal systems may involve a bifurcated strategy,
where different optimizations are applied depending on the
specific application’s needs. One approach may be deployed
for creating general-purpose embeddings, while another,
more specialized one is used for high-speed, large-scale
information retrieval.
V. COMPARATIVE ANALYSIS OF STRENGTHS AND
WEAKNESSES
Each open-source embedding model discussed comes with
its own strengths and trade-offs, making them suitable for
different NLP tasks and application scenarios as can be seen
in Table 3. For instance, the Word embedding models such
as Word2Vec, GloVe, and FastText are widely recognized for
their efficiency in processing large datasets and their ability to
capture semantic relationships between words. These models
generate embeddings based on word co-occurrence patterns,
making them relatively fast to train, particularly in the case
of Word2Vec and FastText. However, a key limitation of
these models is that they produce static embeddings. This
means a word is always represented by the same vector,
regardless of the context in which it appears. For example,
the word ‘‘bank’’ would have the same embedding whether
it’s used in the sentence ‘‘He went to the bank to withdraw
cash’’ or ‘‘The river overflowed its bank.’’ This lack of
contextual awareness can reduce accuracy in tasks requiring
nuanced understanding. Among these, FastText offers an
edge by incorporating subword information (character n-
grams), which allows it to handle out-of-vocabulary words
and capture morphological variations. This is particularly
useful for morphologically rich languages or when dealing
with noisy, user-generated content such as social media text
or OCR output. However, this added capability increases
memory usage compared to Word2Vec and GloVe.
Transformer-based sentence embedding models, such as
BERT, Sentence-BERT, MPNet, and MiniLM, have signif-
icantly advanced the ability to capture contextual meaning
in natural language processing. BERT and MPNet exhibit
strong performance across a wide array of NLP tasks,
demonstrating their versatility. Sentence-BERT, specifically
optimized for generating high-quality sentence embeddings,
excels in tasks involving semantic similarity. In contrast,
MiniLM, developed through knowledge distillation, strikes a
balance between computational efficiency and performance.
However, a common drawback of these models, particularly
BERT and MPNet, is their substantial computational cost,

The reliance on established benchmarks like the MTEB is no

and challenging, including instruction following, long-

largest multilingual collection of evaluation tasks to date.
To facilitate accessibility and reduce computational costs,
MMTEB employs a novel downsampling method based on
inter-task correlation, ensuring a diverse selection of tasks
while preserving the relative model rankings.

B. LONG-CONTEXT CAPABILITIES: NEW BENCHMARKS
AND CHALLENGES
The ability of models to process and understand long contexts
is a crucial capability for real-world applications such as
legal review and financial analysis. Early benchmarks, such
as ‘‘Needle-in-a-Haystack,’’ [71] provided only a ‘‘surface
form of long-context understanding’’ by simply embedding
a key phrase within irrelevant noise. This simplification does
not reflect the complex, multi-document reasoning required
in real-world scenarios.
To address this, new and more sophisticated bench-
marks have been developed. Datasets like L-Eval [72] and
LongBench [73] offer a significant step forward, covering
diverse fields from law to economics and including both
English and Chinese texts. However, the most recent and
advanced benchmark is Loong [74], which is specifically
designed for extended multi-document question answering.
The core principle of Loong is to ‘‘leave no document
behind,’’ meaning every document in the context is relevant
to the final answer, forcing the model to process the entire
context and synthesize information from multiple sources.
Loong includes four novel task types—Spotlight Locating,
Comparison, Clustering, and Chain of Reasoning, that
evaluate different facets of multi-document understanding.
The progression from LongBench to Loong signifies a
critical shift from simple information retrieval to multi-
document reasoning. The former primarily tests a model’s
ability to recall information from a long document, while
the latter, with its ‘‘Chain of Reasoning’’ task, assesses
the model’s capacity to perform multi-step, cross-document
inference. This mirrors the trend in the broader LLM field
toward massive context windows and demonstrates that
evaluation methodologies are evolving to keep pace with the
increasingly complex cognitive tasks that modern models are
capable of performing.
As illustrated in Table 5, modern open-source models
demonstrate a clear correlation between parameter scale
and their proficiency in complex reasoning tasks. While
smaller models like All-MiniLM-L6-v2 remain efficient for
short-form semantic similarity, they lack the architectural
capacity for the ‘‘Chain of Reasoning’’ and ‘‘Spotlight Locat-
ing’’ tasks central to the Loong benchmark. Performance
is primarily quantified via the nDCG@10 (Normalized
Discounted Cumulative Gain at rank 10) metric. This metric
is critical for long-context evaluation as it rewards models
for placing highly relevant documents at the top of the
retrieval list, applying a logarithmic discount to relevant items
appearing at lower ranks to reflect the limited processing
window of downstream LLMs.
C. MEASURING COMPUTATIONAL AND INFERENTIAL
EFFICIENCY
The definition of ‘‘efficiency’’ in the context of embedding
models has expanded beyond simple metrics like FLOPs
(Floating-Point Operations Per Second) or training time. With
the shift to LLMs for embedding tasks, inference costs have
become a ‘‘persistent concern’’. To address this, researchers
are exploring various strategies to reduce the size and
complexity of models while maintaining performance. These
include pruning, which removes unnecessary parameters;
quantization, which reduces numerical precision (e.g., from
32-bit to 8-bit); and knowledge distillation, which trains a
smaller model to mimic a larger one.
Latency and model size are also critical metrics. Models
like Voyage-3-lite exemplify a pragmatic approach to deploy-
ment, achieving a strong cost-to-performance ratio with a
smaller output size of 512 dimensions. This architectural
choice leads to proportionally faster searches compared

D. LIMITATIONS OF BENCHMARK EVALUATION
While benchmarks like MTEB provide a valuable tool for
comparing and evaluating the performance of embedding
models, it’s crucial to acknowledge their limitations. Relying
solely on benchmark results can sometimes lead to a
skewed perception of a model’s true capabilities and its
suitability for real-world applications. One key limitation
is that benchmarks may not cover all relevant downstream
tasks [75]. Benchmarks are designed to assess performance
across a range of common NLP tasks, but they might not
include the specific tasks or nuances of every application [76].
A model that performs well on a general benchmark might not
necessarily excel in a highly specialized or domain-specific
task. However, newer benchmarks, such as BEIR [77] and
those based on SuperGLUE principles [78], are actively being
developed to mitigate this overfitting by incorporating more
diverse and challenging evaluation scenarios. These bench-
marks emphasize robustness and generalization, offering a
more reliable assessment of embedding model quality.
Furthermore, benchmarks can be susceptible to overfit-
ting [79]. Developers might inadvertently optimize their
models to perform well on specific benchmark datasets,
potentially sacrificing generalization ability [80]. This can
result in models that achieve high scores on benchmarks
but fail to perform as effectively when applied to unseen
data or real-world scenarios. Evaluation metrics used in
benchmarks may also not fully capture the nuances of
semantic understanding. While metrics like accuracy, F1-
score, and cosine similarity provide quantitative measures of
performance, they might not always reflect the subtle aspects
of language comprehension that are crucial for many NLP
applications [81].
It is also important to consider the potential biases present
in benchmark datasets [82], [83]. If the datasets used for
evaluation contain inherent biases related to gender, race,
or other sensitive attributes, the benchmark results might
not accurately reflect a model’s fairness or its potential for
generating biased outcomes. Addressing these limitations
requires a more holistic approach to evaluation, combining
benchmark results with thorough testing on real-world data
and careful consideration of ethical implications.
VII. CHALLENGES IN USING EMBEDDING MODELS
While embedding models have proven to be incredibly
powerful tools in NLP, their application is not without its
challenges and potential pitfalls. One significant concern is
the issue of bias [83]. Embedding models are trained on vast
amounts of text data, and if this data contains societal biases
related to gender, race, or other demographic attributes, these
biases can be inadvertently encoded into the vector represen-
tations learned by the model [82]. This can lead to skewed
or unfair outcomes in downstream applications, such as
search results that disproportionately favor certain groups or
recommendation systems that reinforce existing stereotypes.
Addressing bias in embedding models is therefore a critical
ethical consideration, requiring careful data curation and the
development of bias mitigation techniques. Several strategies
can be employed to detect and mitigate bias. Detection
methods include analyzing embeddings for skewed repre-
sentations of gender, race, or other sensitive attributes using
metrics like the Word Embedding Association Test (WEAT)
or similar bias measurement techniques [84]. Mitigation
strategies can be broadly categorized into: Pre-processing
techniques, which involve modifying the training data to
balance representation or removing biased examples; In-
processing techniques, which modify the training objective
or model architecture to reduce bias during training, such as
adversarial debiasing; and post-processing techniques, which
adjust the learned embeddings after training to remove biased
information, like geometric debiasing.
Another challenge arises from the dimensionality of the
embeddings produced by these models. Modern embedding
models often generate vectors with hundreds or even
thousands of dimensions (e.g., 768, 1024, or 1536 dimensions
are common). While higher dimensionality can enable the
capture of richer semantic information, it also leads to
increased storage requirements and computational costs [85].
Furthermore, very high-dimensional data can sometimes
suffer from the ‘‘curse of dimensionality,’’ where the
performance of certain machine learning algorithms degrades

VIII. FINETUNING STRATEGIES
Embedding models traditionally assume static environments;
however, real-world systems, particularly in robotics and
knowledge-intensive applications, often operate in dynamic
settings where new entities and relations continuously
emerge. This discrepancy introduces significant challenges,
notably the need for frequent retraining and the phenomenon
of catastrophic forgetting, where models lose previously
acquired knowledge when exposed to new data. To address
these limitations, recent research has explored continual
learning and efficient finetuning strategies that enable
embeddings to adapt incrementally without full retraining,
while also investigating causal approaches to enhance
interpretability and robustness.

A. CONTINUAL LEARNING FOR EMBEDDINGS
Traditional knowledge graph embedding (KGE) models such
as TransE and Analogy operate under the assumption of a
fixed set of entities and relations, making them ill-suited
for dynamic environments. In scenarios where new facts
are continuously observed, these models require complete
retraining, resulting in high computational overhead and
vulnerability to catastrophic forgetting [91]. To overcome
these challenges, Daruna et al. introduce the Continual
Knowledge Graph Embedding (CKGE) framework, which
integrates continual learning principles into KGE models.
CKGE enables incremental updates of embeddings as new
knowledge becomes available, eliminating the need to retain
all historical data and significantly reducing retraining costs.
One of the central methods explored in CKGE is Deep
Generative Replay (DGR), a technique designed to maintain
stability across learning sessions. As illustrated in Figure 5,
the DGR architecture is based on a Variational Autoencoder
(VAE) framework, where input triples representing head,
relation, and tail entities are embedded and processed through
a Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU)-based encoder. This encoder
projects the input into a latent space that captures semantic
patterns in a compressed form. A corresponding decoder,
composed of GRU layers and a softmax output, reconstructs
prior knowledge from the latent representation, enabling
the system to ‘‘replay’’ previously learned facts without
explicitly storing all past data. Linear transformation layers,
indicated by white outlines in the architecture, facilitate
transitions between embedding spaces, latent variables, and
predictions. Through empirical evaluations on benchmarks
such as WN18RR and FB15K237, Daruna et al. demon-
strated that DGR significantly mitigates forgetting, albeit
at the cost of increased training time compared to simpler
regularization-based methods.
B. EFFICIENT METHODS FOR UPDATING EMBEDDINGS
Beyond DGR, several efficient techniques have been pro-
posed to update embeddings in dynamic environments with-
out exhaustive retraining. Regularization-based approaches
such as L2 regularization and Synaptic Intelligence selec-
tively constrain parameter updates, penalizing deviations
from previously important weights and thereby preserving
earlier knowledge [91]. Architectural innovations like Pro-
gressive Neural Networks and Copy Weight Re-initialization
further enhance continual learning by allocating separate net-
work components for new tasks or by strategically resetting
weights to integrate new information. These methods are
particularly valuable in resource-constrained settings, where
storage, computation, and time are limited. Collectively, they
make incremental embedding updates practical and scalable,
supporting real-time applications in fields like robotics,
recommendation systems, and edge computing.
C. CAUSAL EMBEDDINGS FOR INTERPRETABILITY AND
ROBUSTNESS
While continual learning addresses the dynamics of evolving
knowledge, another emerging frontier focuses on embedding
causal reasoning directly into model architectures. Causal
embeddings aim to move beyond the predictive capabil-
ities of traditional models by explicitly modeling causal
relationships, thus enabling interventions and counterfac-
tual reasoning. Unlike standard deep learning models that
act as closed boxes, causal embeddings provide greater
transparency and fairness by answering ‘‘what-if’’ scenarios
based on learned causal structures [92]. Li et al. introduced
causal embedding frameworks tailored for recommendation
systems. In their work on ‘‘Multimodal Graph Causal Embed-
ding for Multimedia-based Recommendation,’’ they leverage
multimodal data sources to model causal dependencies
between entities, improving both recommendation accuracy
and system interpretability. Similarly, their CausalCDR

framework for cross-domain recommendation embeds causal
knowledge to facilitate the transfer of insights across domains
while maintaining fairness and reliability. The architecture
of causal embeddings typically involves processing raw
input features through encoders that extract high-level causal
concepts, which are then used for outcome prediction and
counterfactual analysis. By embedding causal reasoning into
the learning process, models become more robust against
biases and more transparent to end users, especially in sen-
sitive domains such as healthcare, finance, and personalized
recommendations.
IX. APPLICATIONS OF EMBEDDING MODELS IN
VARIOUS DOMAINS
Embedding models have found widespread applications
across a multitude of domains, demonstrating their versatility
and power in understanding and processing natural language.
In search engines, embedding models enable semantic search
capabilities, allowing users to find relevant information
even when their queries use different keywords that have
the same underlying meaning [93], [94]. By matching the
semantic meaning of a query with the semantic meaning
of documents, search engines can provide more accurate
and comprehensive results. Recommendation systems also
heavily rely on embedding models to understand user
preferences [95], [96]. By representing user interests and item
characteristics as embeddings, these systems can identify
semantic similarities and provide more personalized and
relevant recommendations. In the realm of security, fraud
detection can benefit from embedding models by represent-
ing user behavior or transaction patterns as vectors [97].
Anomalous patterns in the embedding space can then be
identified as potential indicators of fraudulent activity [98].
The healthcare domain has also seen increasing adoption
of embedding models for tasks like medical diagnosis [99],
[100]. By applying these models to medical texts such
as patient records, research papers, and clinical notes,
it becomes possible to identify similar cases, extract crucial
information, and potentially assist in the diagnostic process.
Specialized models like BioBERT and ClinicalBERT, which
are BERT variants trained on biomedical text, are particularly
valuable in this field [101], [102]. Information retrieval is
a critical area where embedding models significantly con-
tribute, particularly in the context of Retrieval-Augmented
Generation (RAG) systems [103], [104]. In RAG systems,
embedding models are used to retrieve relevant documents
or passages from a large corpus based on a user’s query.
The retrieved information is then integrated into the input
of large language models, enhancing their ability to generate
more accurate, contextual, and informative responses. This
retrieval capability is also essential for clustering and classi-
fication tasks, where embedding models group semantically
similar data points, enabling efficient content organization.
These tasks support applications such as sentiment analysis,
topic modeling, and document categorization [105]. While
machine translation is not the primary focus for most
embedding models, they play a crucial role in representing
the meaning of text in different languages, with multilingual
embedding models being essential for cross-lingual tasks.
The practical applications of open-source embedding
models can be demonstrated through several examples across
various domains. For instance, in semantic search, models
like Sentence-BERT (e.g., All-Mpnet-Base-v2 ) are used
to generate embeddings for documents within a knowledge
base. When a user submits a query, the model compares the
query’s embedding to those of the documents, retrieving the
most semantically relevant articles. In recommendation sys-
tems, models like Word2Vec or FastText learn embeddings
for products based on user purchase history. By measuring the
similarity between the embeddings of previously purchased
items and other products, the system can suggest new items
that align with the user’s preferences. In the case of sentiment
analysis, a BERT model can be fine-tuned on a labeled
dataset of customer reviews to predict the sentiment of
unseen reviews. Similarly, in question answering systems
utilizing RAG, high-performance models like MPNet or BGE
can retrieve relevant context from a document collection,
providing the necessary information for a large language
model to generate an accurate response. Lastly, in the medical
domain, models like BioBERT can be applied to patient
notes to find similar cases, aiding in diagnosing conditions
and planning treatments by identifying patterns from related
patient data.
A. THE SHIFT TO GENERATIVE RETRIEVAL
The most profound re-engineering of the retrieval process
is the emergence of generative retrieval [106], [107]. This
cutting-edge approach, showcased by models like GENIUS,
represents a paradigm shift away from traditional vector
search. Conventional embedding-based methods map queries
and database items into a high-dimensional space and then
perform a nearest-neighbor search to find the most similar
vectors. This approach, however, faces significant scalability
challenges, as the cost of building and maintaining indexes
grows with the size of the dataset, leading to slower retrieval
times for large collections.
In contrast, generative retrieval, as demonstrated by the
GENIUS model presented at the 2025 Conference on
Computer Vision and Pattern Recognition (CVPR) [108],
eliminates the need for expensive index building. Instead, the
generative model directly outputs the unique identifier (ID)
of the target data item. This enables constant-time retrieval,
meaning the speed of the search remains constant regardless
of the database size. The model achieves this through two core
innovations: semantic quantization, which creates a sequence
of codes as a target ID, and query augmentation, which helps
the model generalize to new data by interpolating between
initial queries and target IDs.
This transformation challenges the very foundation of
current Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) systems.

become the entire retrieval engine. This means that future
multimodal systems may move beyond traditional vector
databases, relying instead on highly efficient generative
models that can produce direct, quantifiable search results.
This represents a significant step forward in optimizing
both the accuracy and efficiency of multimodal information
retrieval.
X. LIMITATIONS
This survey provides a structured, deployment-aware per-
spective on open-source embedding models while operating
within several defined boundaries. Rather than establishing
definitive rankings, it treats benchmark results as time-
dependent snapshots, acknowledging that performance is
highly sensitive to evaluation protocols and domain-specific
characteristics. The analysis of efficiency, scalability, and
operational trade-offs is derived from published reports and
documentation rather than a unified, controlled experimental
evaluation, meaning that variables such as hardware config-
urations, numerical precision, and batch settings may limit
strict comparability. Furthermore, the scope is constrained
by the inconsistency of technical reporting across the lit-
erature, particularly regarding training data, ‘‘open-source’’
licensing nuances, and the emerging field of multimodal
representations which can impact traceability. Ultimately, the
work discusses practical deployment, bias, and robustness at
a conceptual and synthesis-driven level rather than through
primary empirical validation, highlighting ongoing research
challenges in standardizing evaluations across heterogeneous
tasks and maintaining representation quality under domain
shift.
XI. CONCLUSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
This study has explored the rich landscape of open-source
embedding models, highlighting their definition, signifi-
cance, diverse types, and the crucial role of benchmarks in
their evaluation. A comprehensive list of prominent open-
source models, ranging from foundational word embeddings
like Word2Vec and GloVe to advanced contextual and
multimodal embeddings such as BERT, Sentence-BERT,
MPNet, MiniLM, BGE, GTE, and Nomic Embed, has
been presented. Each model exhibits unique strengths
and weaknesses, making them suitable for different NLP
tasks and application scenarios. Benchmark results from
platforms like MTEB provide valuable insights into their
relative performance on tasks such as text classification,
semantic similarity, and information retrieval, underscoring
the influence of factors like training data, model size, and
architecture on their effectiveness. Common pitfalls and chal-
lenges associated with using embedding models, including
bias, dimensionality issues, sensitivity to input variations,
and contextual limitations, necessitate careful consideration
and the application of appropriate mitigation strategies.
The wide-ranging applications of embedding models across
domains like search engines, recommendation systems,
fraud detection, medical diagnosis, information retrieval,
and content organization demonstrate their transformative
potential in various industries. Furthermore, the ability
of embedding models to enhance documentation through
semantic search, content organization, improved linking, and
personalized experiences highlights their value in creating
more effective and user-friendly information resources.
Beyond benchmark performance, an important insight
from this survey is that embedding model selection should
be treated as an engineering decision rather than a purely
accuracy-driven choice. Marginal improvements in bench-
mark scores often come at a disproportionate cost in latency,
memory footprint, and operational complexity, particularly
for large transformer-based models. Therefore, it is required
to make a reasonable tradeoff between performance metrics,
memory footprint, and operational complexity, and energy
consumption to ensure acceptable performance, lower cost
and to satisfy sustainability requirements.
Selecting the most suitable open-source embedding model
for a specific need requires careful consideration of several
factors. The primary driver should be the specific NLP
task at hand, as different models are optimized for different
applications. For tasks requiring fine-grained semantic under-
standing at the word level, traditional word embeddings might
suffice, while tasks involving contextual understanding of
sentences or documents generally benefit from transformer-
based models. The need for multilingual support is another
critical factor, with models like BGE and GTE offering
strong capabilities in this area. Computational efficiency and
resource constraints should also be considered, as larger,
more powerful models often come with higher computational
costs. The length of the input text can also influence model
selection, with newer models offering larger context windows
for handling longer documents. Ultimately, the desired level
of accuracy and the specific requirements of the application
will guide the choice.
In practice, efficient sentence-level embeddings such
as MiniLM-, MPNet-, or Nomic-based models frequently
provide superior cost–performance tradeoffs for produc-
tion systems, including retrieval-augmented generation and
semantic search pipelines. High-capacity models are best
reserved for accuracy-critical scenarios where latency and
memory constraints are secondary. It is often beneficial to
experiment with a few different models and evaluate their
performance on a relevant dataset. In many cases, fine-tuning
a pre-trained model on domain-specific data can lead to
significant improvements in performance, making this an
important step in optimizing the use of embedding models
for particular applications.
Finally, this survey highlights the need for future research
to move beyond accuracy-centric evaluation by incorpo-
rating standardized efficiency-aware benchmarks, broader
multilingual and multimodal assessments, and adaptive
embedding strategies that explicitly balance precision and
computational cost at inference time. More fundamentally,
the field lacks standardized, hardware-aware evaluation

energy consumption, and latency under realistic deployment

multilingual equity, multimodal consistency, and robustness
under domain shift—remains a central research priority for
the next generation of embedding systems.
