ABSTRACT
In this industry track presentation, we will provide a comprehen-
sive tour of the best performing embedding models for question
answering, as determined by the Massive Text Embedding Bench-
mark.1 We showcase these models while also considering solutions
offered by OpenAI and Cohere, renowned for their state-of-the-art
performance. Through rigorous evaluations on internal Salesforce
datasets tailored for Question Answering on Knowledge articles,
we compare the performance of these models using standardized
metrics.
Our analysis sheds light on the current state-of-the-art in ques-
tion answering using embedding models across three diverse do-
mains. We hope that this talk’s outcomes will empower practition-
ers and researchers to make informed decisions when selecting the
most suitable solution for their specific requirements.

1 INTRODUCTION
In this talk, we present a comparison of embedding models for
question answering (QnA), focusing on their performance in real-
world QnA scenarios. The best practises and the lessons learned
shared in this talk stem from deploying a QnA system in production
as part of Salesforce Search on Knowledge articles. The system
today serves several thousands of QnA queries daily.
A high-level version of the QnA pipeline architecture in pro-
duction is shown in Figure 1. It is inspired from [1] where it is
shown that it can be highly beneficial to combine sparse and dense
retrieval. In the talk we will highlight the major parts of the ar-
chitecture enumerating its advantages and some of the challenges
when putting it in production at scale.
To shed light on the impact of embedding models we will use
three human-annotated, internal datasets from different profes-
sional domains ranging from manufacturing to job portals. We will

2 PROBLEM SETTING
Salesforce Customer Relations Management (CRM) organises cus-
tomer data under different objects depending on their type. Some
examples include Accounts, which are commonly used to store
organisations and Opportunities, which hold information about
business deals. One of these objects is the Knowledge object, which
lets customers create their knowledge base. The Knowledge base
of each customer is built from knowledge articles, which are doc-
uments of information. Articles can include information on pro-
cesses, like how to reset a product to its defaults, or frequently
asked questions, like how much storage a product supports.
The specificity of knowledge articles compared to records of
other standard objects of Salesforce CRM is that they are long text
documents.2 As a counter-example, Accounts mostly contain struc-
tured information as key-values, like forms. Due to this, Knowledge
articles can be used to build modern NLP products, like QnA that
aims to reduce the effort of users when seeking information.
A major challenge when building such NLP products at Sales-
force scale is the requirement to perform well across thousands
of customers. Customer data are kept separate in a multi-tenant
environment: one can not pool data across customers to train or
fine-tune global models that use data from other customers. Also,
any deployed solution needs to perform well across customers
whose data are different: Salesforce has customers that use very
different vocabulary whose distribution is often very different from
publicly available resources like Wikipedia.
3 QUESTION ANSWERING PIPELINE
A high-level version of the online (inference) QnA pipeline archi-
tecture in production is shown in Figure 1. It is inspired from [1]
where it is shown that it can be highly beneficial to combine sparse
and dense retrieval. The indexing pipeline which pre-processes the
knowledge articles and hydrates the sparse and the dense indexes
is not shown here due to the lack of space. An important detail is
that as in [1], articles are split in passages. Passages are small text
spans that respect minimum and maximum length limits as well as
the HTML format of the article.
In the online pipeline of Figure 1 each component functions as
follows:
• Dense Retrieval: a query is transformed to a dense vector
using an embedding model. The vector is issued as a query in
a dense index and the most relevant passages are retrieved.

a “Fusion Ranker” model that combines their scores. The reranked list of scores is further processed in the the “Postprocessing”
module which applies business logic and decides between Show/No Show for the top-1 result.
• Sparse Retrieval: a query is transformed to a sparse vector
using a scheme like BM25. The vector is issued as a query in
a sparse index and the most relevant passages are retrieved.
• Fusion Reranker: The ordered lists of sparse and dense
passages are merged using a combination of their scores.
• Postprocessing: The merged list of ranked passages is pro-
cessed and business logic is applied. Depending on a score
threshold that acts as a cut-off a passage may be shown as
result to the Search Result Page (SERP) module.

There are several observations on the table that we hope will
be the basis for fruitful and interactive discussions in the industry
day:
• The comparison between sparse and dense retrieval methods.
In particular, depending on the domain (e.g, Domain C),
sparse representations are very competitive.
• The trade-offs between open-source models and embedding
providers. Notice, for example, that the gap between OpenAI
and open-source models closes.
• Extensions of this architecture to improve the QnA user
experience. LLMs can play an important role for this.
There are other lessons learned from putting the QnA system in
production we plan to share as part of the talk:
• Generating quality passages from the article HTML is not
trivial. While HTML tags help, exclusively relying on them
is not sufficient.
• Embedding models progress fast: to benefit model details
need to be abstracted out from the design.
• Correlating online with offline metrics for products whose
success means good abandonment (no click) is challenging.
